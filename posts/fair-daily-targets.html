Setting fair daily targets when every task isn't equal

A flat daily quota looks fair on paper and feels unfair in practice. Here's the weighted-target model that fixed it — with the formula, the edge cases, and what the team said afterward.

The problem with flat quotas

Imagine a processing team where every person handles a different mix of task types. Some tasks take four minutes; others take forty. If everyone gets the same daily target — say, 60 items — the people assigned heavier task mixes fall behind through no fault of their own, while others coast.

The team knows it. Morale data shows it. But leadership still needs one number per person per day. The answer isn't removing targets — it's weighting them.

The weighted-target model

The core idea: a person's daily target should reflect the throughput rate of each task type they handle, weighted by how much of their day each type occupies.

Daily Target = Σ ( Rate per hour × % of task mix ) × Working hours

Three inputs, all measurable from historical data:

  • Rate per hour — how many items of each task type an average performer completes per hour
  • Task mix % — what share of the person's assigned volume each type represents
  • Working hours — productive hours in the shift after breaks and meetings (7 is a common real-world number for an 8-hour shift)

Worked example with demo data

Say a team member's assignment for the month looks like this:

  • Type A: 12 items/hour, 50% of their volume
  • Type B: 6 items/hour, 30% of their volume
  • Type C: 3 items/hour, 20% of their volume

Their weighted rate is (12 × 0.5) + (6 × 0.3) + (3 × 0.2) = 6 + 1.8 + 0.6 = 8.4 items/hour. Over a 7-hour productive day, that's a target of 59 items — while a colleague with a lighter mix might correctly get 75. Different numbers, same effort. That's the point.

Edge cases that will bite you

  • Experience tiers. New joiners shouldn't carry the same rate as veterans. A simple multiplier by tenure grade (e.g. 1.0× / 1.1× / 1.2×) keeps it fair without a separate model.
  • Exception time. Some share of the day always goes to rework, queries, and interruptions. Build in an adjustable exception percentage rather than pretending it's zero.
  • Mix drift. Task mix changes month to month. Recalculate targets on the same cycle the mix is assigned, or the model quietly goes stale.
Note: All figures in this post are demonstration data. The model structure is what transfers — plug in your own team's measured rates.

What happened after rollout

The most telling change wasn't in the production numbers — it was in the conversations. Disputes about targets dropped because the target was now explainable: anyone could see exactly which rates and mix percentages produced their number. Fairness you can audit beats fairness you have to trust.
